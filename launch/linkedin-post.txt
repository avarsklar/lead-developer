The launch-readiness check failed my own app last month. I'm the one who wrote the check.

The error monitor said "active" in every dashboard. Then the check did what dashboards don't: threw a fake error on purpose and watched for the alarm. Nothing arrived. The monitor had been silently dead in production — if a real user had hit a real bug, nobody would have known for days.

That check exists because I hit a wall I think a lot of people are hitting right now. I built a campus marketplace with AI as a college student. It worked. Real people used it. And then I got scared to change a single line, because I might break the thing people actually depended on — and I had no way back.

So I spent this summer building the thing I needed: Lead Developer, a free, open-source kit of nine plain-English skills that turn the AI you already build with into a careful senior developer for the app you're scared to touch. You run one command and it tells you which safety net you're missing and the single next move.

The whole kit runs on one rule: a safety net isn't real until you've watched it catch something. We stage a fake failure on a private practice copy — your real app is never touched — and you watch the net catch it. The first time I rehearsed my own emergency plan this way, the drill caught a real bug in my own rollback script. The thing meant to save me on the worst night would have failed exactly when I needed it.

One honest limit: the automated skills need Claude Code (a paid plan). The plain-language handbook — the whole method, with a short video per skill — is free forever, whatever tool you build with.

If you've built something real with AI and you're scared to touch it, the handbook starts with a four-question check that names which gate you're at: https://avarsklar.github.io/lead-developer/
