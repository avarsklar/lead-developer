I built an app for my college campus for fun. It's called Bucknell Refit, a marketplace where students buy and sell to each other. I made it because I wanted an easier way to sell my own clothes. AI let me build it faster than I expected, it worked, and students actually started using it.

Then it stopped being fun. Real people were on it, real money was moving through it, their personal information was sitting in my database, and I had no idea how to keep any of it safe. I couldn't fix one button without worrying I'd crash the whole site. I could build the app, but I had no idea how to run it.

AI is incredible at getting you from nothing to a working app. It just doesn't give you the part that comes after "it works," which is exactly the part I was suddenly responsible for.

So instead of trying to become an engineer overnight, I did the one thing I already knew how to do. I got the same AI I build with to act like a careful senior developer instead of an eager intern. It copies the app before touching anything, tries every change on a private version first, shows me, and only goes live when I say okay, always keeping a way back.

I put everything I worked out into a free set of skills, built with two Rice University professors: a step-by-step ladder from "scared to touch it" to "running it like a real thing."

The handbook is free here: https://avarsklar.github.io/lead-developer/
The skills are free on GitHub: https://github.com/avarsklar/lead-developer-skills
