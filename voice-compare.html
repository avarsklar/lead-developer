…or record your own

For a guide that's about calming scared people, your own warm voice might beat any of these. It's easy to wire in:

  1. Record nine short clips (Voice Memos, QuickTime, anything) — one per skill, ~15–20 seconds each.
  2. Name each file by its skill: readiness-check.m4a, safety-net.m4a, … (any common audio format is fine).
  3. Drop them in a folder and tell me where. I'll clean them up, sync each to its video, and re-render.

Here are the exact lines to read — same words the videos use now:

readiness-check — Not sure where to start? Run readiness check. You answer four easy questions, it reads your app for you, and it hands you which gate you're at, and the single next move to make. A map you never had.

safety-net — Safety net builds a floor you can't fall through. A saved version you can return to in one move. Your secret keys cleaned out of your code. And automatic backups, with a restore you actually watch work. After this, you cannot lose your work, or your data.

ship-change — Ship change lets you change anything, safely. Your live app is set aside, untouched. You try the change on a private copy first, look at it, and it only goes live when you say so. With a one-move way back, every single time.

qa-harness — How do you know nothing broke? QA harness turns a flow that must never break into a check. You watch it go red on purpose, once. After that, it checks itself on every change. So green means proven, not hoped.

go-live-check — Before real strangers arrive, go live check proves your safety nets actually work. It makes each alarm fail on purpose, and you watch the alert land on your phone. So you find out the moment something breaks. Not from an angry email.

emergency-plan — Emergency plan is your calm plan for the night it breaks. A one-page card, on every device. Is it just me, or everyone? How do I get back to working? What do I tell my users? Who do I call? The plan isn't trapped in your panicked head anymore.

release-foundation — Release foundation puts your new features on a calendar. You pick how often you release, and line your features up by date. A finished feature waits in a holding pen until its day. Miss the train? It just catches the next one.

release — On release day, release puts your parked feature live, with the seatbelts on. It catches the feature up, runs your checks, and you watch them go green. Then, on your okay, it flips on. Bookmarked, and written down. The scary work happened days ago.

handoff — Handoff makes sure you're not the only one who can run it. It writes down where everything lives, a first-day guide for a new operator, and then proves it. A stranger runs a real task from your notes alone. Your app is an asset now, not a leash.
